Intercom is better if you want proactive in-app messaging and product tours. Zendesk is better if you need ticket routing and SLA management at scale. That's the short answer. The longer answer involves pricing that behaves nothing like the pricing page, AI costs that spike without warning, and reporting that stops exactly where the interesting questions start. Both will cost you more than advertised, and neither was built for DTC ecommerce. This post has the pricing gotchas, the real user complaints, and six alternatives that the affiliate-funded comparison posts usually leave out.

## The comparison table

| | Intercom | Zendesk |
|---|---|---|
| Starting price | $29/seat/mo (Essential) | $19/agent/mo (Support Team) |
| Mid-tier | $85/seat/mo (Advanced) | $115/agent/mo (Suite Professional) |
| AI pricing | Fin: $0.99 per resolution | Advanced AI add-on: $50/agent/mo |
| Best for | Product-led SaaS, in-app messaging | High-volume ticket ops, enterprise |
| Ecommerce integrations | Shopify is view-only. Stripe doesn't support multiple accounts. | 1,500+ marketplace apps, but not built for ecommerce. |
| Reporting | Looks great, goes an inch deep. 120-event tracking cap. | Deep on tickets and agents. Blind to revenue, LTV, acquisition. |
| Review scores (G2 / Trustpilot) | 4.5 / 1.9 | 4.3 / 1.6 |

The Trustpilot scores are worth paying attention to. G2 reviews skew toward people evaluating tools. Trustpilot skews toward people paying for them.

## Where Intercom wins

Intercom's strength is that it treats support as part of the product, not something bolted on afterward. Product tours, in-app messaging, targeted campaigns based on user behavior. If you want to onboard users and answer questions inside your app without pushing them to a separate help page, Intercom does that well.

Fin AI is the standout feature. It handles common questions autonomously and does a genuinely good job of it compared to most chatbot implementations I've tested. The API is well-documented, developer-friendly, and one of the better ones in the support tool category.

Best fit: SaaS companies with product-led growth motions, teams under 20 agents who want support woven into the product experience itself. If your support model is reactive ticket management, Intercom is overbuilt for how you'll use it. You'll end up paying for product tour infrastructure and in-app messaging that never gets configured.

### The complaints people actually have about Intercom

Intercom's Trustpilot rating is 1.9 out of 5 across 950+ reviews. Compare that to their 4.5 on G2 and you start to see a pattern. The people evaluating Intercom love it. The people paying for it month after month have a different experience.

Reliability is a real concern. One G2 reviewer reported four outages lasting over a day in a single month, with customer submissions lost during the downtime. For a tool that is supposed to be your primary customer communication channel, that's a problem you can't work around. When your support tool goes down, your customers don't stop having problems. They just lose the ability to tell you about them.

Then there's the billing. A user reported their monthly bill jumping from $200 to $1,400 during a product launch because Fin AI resolved (or "resolved") a surge of conversations. The reason that word needs air quotes: Intercom counts "assumed resolutions" where a customer simply gives up and leaves the chat. Fin still charges $0.99 for each of those. You are paying for conversations where nobody actually got helped.

On the data side, the API only syncs the last 500 items. If you need historical data for reporting or migration, you're building workarounds from day one. Want to pull six months of conversation data into a data warehouse? Too bad. You get the last 500 records and nothing else. SaaS Price Pulse analysis shows that actual Intercom spend runs 60-80% above the base seat cost once you factor in Fin usage, overages, and the features that require tier upgrades. For a tool that starts at $29 per seat, the real number is closer to $50-$52 per seat when everything is included.

## Where Zendesk wins

Zendesk is the workhorse. Ticket routing, SLA management, multi-channel support across email, chat, phone, and social. It handles high volume without falling apart, scales to hundreds of agents, and has the operational maturity that comes from being around since 2007.

The marketplace has 1,500+ integrations. If you need to connect Zendesk to something, there's probably an app for it already. For teams running formal support operations with defined escalation paths, shift scheduling, and compliance requirements, Zendesk is built for exactly that workflow.

Best fit: companies with 50+ agents, dedicated support ops staff, enterprise compliance needs. If you have fewer than 10 agents and simple support needs, Zendesk is going to feel like driving a semi truck to the grocery store.

### The complaints people actually have about Zendesk

The most common G2 complaint is what reviewers describe as a "paywall around innovations." CSAT surveys, SLA management, multilingual support. These are features most teams consider table stakes for a support tool, and Zendesk locks them behind higher tiers. You show up thinking you're getting a complete support platform and then discover that the features you actually need cost two or three tiers above where you started.

New AI add-ons make this worse. They're incompatible with legacy pricing plans, which forces teams into expensive tier upgrades just to access them. If you signed up for Zendesk three years ago on an older plan, you may find yourself choosing between losing your grandfathered pricing or losing access to AI features.

The cost adds up fast. Add-ons account for 15-35% of total costs on top of per-agent fees. A Kustomer analysis found that a 50-agent Enterprise setup with two add-ons comes to roughly $161,400 per year. That's not a niche scenario for mid-market companies with real support teams.

Zendesk Explore, their reporting tool, is entirely self-referential. It tracks tickets, agents, and SLAs. It has zero concept of customer revenue, lifetime value, or acquisition channel. You can build detailed reports on how your support team is performing, but you cannot connect that performance to whether it's actually retaining customers or driving revenue. I've talked to operators who built custom dashboards in Looker or Metabase just to get the view that Zendesk should have provided out of the box.

## The pricing traps

Intercom lists three tiers: $29, $85, and $132 per seat per month. On paper, that looks straightforward. In practice, Fin AI at $0.99 per resolution turns your bill into a variable cost that spikes with traffic. A product launch, a bug that generates support volume, a seasonal rush. Any of these can double your Fin charges in a single week. The "assumed resolution" mechanic means you're also paying for conversations where the customer abandoned the chat without getting an answer. Budget 60-80% above the listed seat price if you want to forecast accurately.

Zendesk's pricing has a different shape but the same outcome. Support-only plans run $19-$115 per agent per month. The Suite, which is what most teams actually need for multi-channel support, runs $55-$169. The Advanced AI add-on costs another $50 per agent per month, and it's functionally required if you want AI features since the base tiers don't include them. CSAT surveys and SLA management are paywalled below Suite Professional. Add-ons typically add 15-35% to your total annual spend.

Both platforms share the same problem: the pricing page shows you the floor, not the ceiling. The floor is where solo developers evaluating a tool during a free trial will land. The ceiling is where a 20-person team with real conversation volume ends up twelve months later. I've seen teams budget for $10,000 annually and end up at $18,000 with Intercom, or budget for $15,000 and land at $22,000 with Zendesk. The gap between the listed price and the invoice is where both companies make their money.

## Neither tool answers the question that matters

Intercom can tell you how many conversations happened, how fast agents responded, and whether Fin resolved them. It cannot tell you whether those customers later churned, what they bought, or how they found you in the first place. There's a 120-event cap on custom tracking, which means if you're trying to build any kind of behavioral pipeline, you hit the wall quickly.

Zendesk Explore is deep on support metrics, and that's all it sees. You can build detailed reports on ticket volume, agent performance, and SLA compliance. You cannot answer "did our Q4 support issues contribute to the churn spike in January?" because Zendesk doesn't know what churn is. It doesn't know what revenue is either.

The question most operators actually care about is "how is support quality affecting revenue?" It is unanswerable in both platforms. They are support tools that measure support. Revenue lives in Stripe. Product usage lives in your app. Marketing attribution lives in your ad platforms. None of those connections exist natively. You end up being the human integration layer, pulling CSVs from three systems and trying to find the pattern in a spreadsheet at 11 PM on a Sunday.

## Six alternatives worth considering

### Gorgias, if you're on Shopify

$10-$900 per month depending on ticket volume. Gorgias is Shopify-native, which means agents can refund orders, cancel subscriptions, and edit shipping details without leaving the ticket. Revenue attribution is built in, so you can see how much revenue your support team influenced. G2 rates it 4.6 out of 5.

If you're a DTC brand on Shopify, this is probably what you should be using. It lacks Zendesk's enterprise routing and Intercom's product tour features, but for ecommerce support it's purpose-built in a way that neither Intercom nor Zendesk is.

### Help Scout, if you're small and want to stay simple

$20-$60 per user per month. Used by Hero Cosmetics and Tuft & Needle. Clean interface, human-first design, no enterprise bloat. The onboarding takes hours instead of weeks.

If you have fewer than 10 agents and value simplicity over configurability, start here. You'll outgrow it eventually if you scale past 50 agents, but for small teams it removes the overhead that Zendesk and Intercom pile on. The documentation is clear, the pricing is predictable, and you won't spend your first month just configuring the tool.

### Others worth a look

Front starts at $19 per user and is built around shared inboxes and team collaboration. It works well for high-touch brands where multiple people weigh in on customer issues before responding.

Freshdesk offers 30-60% savings compared to Zendesk for a similar feature set. If your primary constraint is budget and you need Zendesk-style ticket management, it's worth evaluating.

Kustomer includes a full customer timeline with purchase history natively. It is owned by Meta, which some teams flag as a data concern depending on their customer base.

Gladly is omnichannel support designed for mid-market DTC. Worth looking at if you've outgrown Gorgias but don't need the full weight of Zendesk Enterprise. Their pricing model charges per conversation rather than per agent, which can save money if you have a large team handling lower volume.

## The real problem with choosing a support tool

Every support tool comparison ends with "pick the one that fits your needs." That's true but useless. The actual problem is that whichever tool you pick, it becomes another data silo. IDC estimates that companies lose 20-30% of revenue to inefficiencies caused by disconnected data. Gartner puts the number of organizations struggling with data integration at 87%. These aren't abstract numbers. When a customer who filed three support tickets in their first week churns two months later, neither Intercom nor Zendesk will surface that correlation. They can't. They don't have access to your billing data.

Riven connects support data with billing, product usage, and marketing data so that a ticket is part of that customer's full history across every tool you use. The support interaction becomes one signal in a larger pattern instead of a data point trapped inside a single platform. When someone churns, you see the full timeline: the ad that brought them in, the onboarding friction, the tickets they filed, the emails they stopped opening.

> I've spent more time than I'd like to admit comparing support tools. The honest answer is that it barely matters. Intercom, Zendesk, Gorgias, Help Scout. They all handle tickets. The real question is whether your support data stays trapped inside the tool or connects to everything else in your business. That's the decision that actually affects churn.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is Intercom or Zendesk cheaper for a 10-person team?

Zendesk starts lower at $19 per agent versus Intercom's $29 per seat. But once you factor in Intercom's Fin AI charges and Zendesk's add-on fees, actual annual spend for 10 agents lands somewhere between $5,000 and $15,000 for either platform. The pricing pages make them look dramatically different. The invoices don't.

### Can you migrate from one to the other?

Both offer migration tools, but expect two to four weeks of cleanup. Custom fields, formatting, and internal notes transfer imperfectly. Macros and automations need to be rebuilt from scratch in most cases. The subscription cost difference between the two platforms is usually smaller than the labor cost of switching, so factor in the transition effort before you commit. I've seen teams spend more on the migration project than they saved in the first year of the new tool.

### Which has better AI features in 2026?

Intercom's Fin AI is more mature for autonomous conversation resolution at $0.99 per resolution. Zendesk's Advanced AI add-on ($50 per agent per month) focuses on agent assistance and routing rather than full automation. Which one costs less depends entirely on your conversation volume. High volume favors Zendesk's flat per-agent fee. Low volume favors Intercom's per-resolution model.

### Is Gorgias better than both for ecommerce?

For Shopify-based DTC brands, yes. It is the only platform where agents can process refunds, cancellations, and order edits directly inside tickets. G2 rates it 4.6 out of 5. It doesn't have Zendesk's enterprise depth or Intercom's product tour capabilities, but if you sell products on Shopify, it is purpose-built for exactly that. The pricing model (based on ticket volume rather than per-seat) also tends to work better for ecommerce teams where support volume is seasonal.
